Diagnostic
What I walk into, and what I do about it

Most organisations I have joined are dealing with variations of the same core problems. These are not unique failures, they are patterns that emerge when technology grows faster than the governance around it. Here is what each one looks like in practice, how I approach it, and what changes as a result.

01
+40%Visibility gain

Nobody knows who owns what

What I walk into Applications sit in a grey zone between the business and IT. When something goes wrong, both sides point at each other. Decisions about investment, change, or risk sit unresolved for months because there is no named person with the authority to make them.
What I do I establish a clear ownership structure, every application gets a named business owner who is accountable for its value, its cost, and its risk. I then build a working model between those owners and the technical teams so that decisions have a home and escalations have a path.
How I do it I start with a RACI across the full application landscape, run alignment sessions with business and IT leadership, and formalise ownership in ServiceNow. Regular governance forums replace the informal back-channels that were causing delays. The ITIL ownership model gives it structure without making it bureaucratic.
Asset Owner Governance RACI Framework Accountability Model Demand Management ITIL
02
60+Apps rationalised

The portfolio has no strategic direction

What I walk into Applications have been added over the years but rarely removed. Nobody has sat down and asked which ones are actually earning their keep. Duplication is rife, some platforms are technically obsolete, and others carry regulatory risk that nobody has formally acknowledged.
What I do I run a structured portfolio assessment, scoring every application against business value, technical health, running cost, and risk exposure. The output is not a report that sits on a shelf. It is a prioritised investment roadmap with concrete decisions: what to invest in, what to maintain, what to migrate, and what to retire.
How I do it I use the Gartner TIME model as the scoring framework. Tolerate, Invest, Migrate, Eliminate. Each application gets a health score, a TCO breakdown, and a business value rating. The full picture goes into a Power BI portfolio heat map that makes the trade-offs visible to leadership without needing a 40-slide deck.
Application Portfolio Management APM Gartner TIME Model TCO Analysis Portfolio Rationalisation Build-Buy-Retire
03
100%ALM coverage

Platforms get deployed and forgotten

What I walk into Systems go live and then drift. Patching is done when something breaks. End-of-life dates are unknown or ignored. Nobody is tracking what stage each application is at, so technical debt quietly builds until it surfaces as an audit finding or an outage.
What I do I put an Application Lifecycle Management framework in place that covers every stage, from initial concept through to active decommission. Each platform in the portfolio has a defined lifecycle stage, a named review owner, and a clear set of criteria for what happens next. Nothing drifts because there is always a next step.
How I do it I use a stage-gate model with governance checkpoints at each transition. Lifecycle status is maintained in ServiceNow CMDB and reviewed quarterly with platform owners. In regulated environments. Pharma GMP previously, financial services now, the same discipline maps directly to DORA operational resilience requirements.
Application Lifecycle Management ALM Stage-Gate Governance CMDB Evergreen Management Decommissioning Strategy Technical Debt
04
75%Cost reduction

Teams are always firefighting, never building

What I walk into Operational demand swallows everything. The same teams responsible for keeping systems running are also meant to be delivering strategic change. Incidents jump the queue, new requests land without a process, and the strategic roadmap never actually moves. Everyone is busy but nothing is progressing.
What I do I separate operational demand from strategic delivery with a proper capacity model. Run activities and change activities get their own allocated capacity, protected from each other. Unplanned work has a defined intake process so it stops bypassing the pipeline. Leadership can see exactly how capacity is being used and where the pressure is building.
How I do it I build a capacity planning model that sets explicit run-versus-change ratios, agreed with leadership. Jira is used to make the flow visible, what is in flight, what is queued, what is blocked. A monthly capacity review with senior stakeholders allows the balance to be adjusted as priorities shift, rather than just reacting to whatever is loudest.
Run-the-Bank vs Change-the-Bank Capacity Planning Demand Management Resource Allocation Portfolio Kanban Intake Governance
05
90%On-time delivery

Regulated platforms running on informal processes

What I walk into Critical platforms, payments, core banking, financial reporting, are being changed and released through informal processes that were never designed to withstand regulatory scrutiny. Compliance is treated as something that happens after delivery, not during it. Audit findings pile up and the teams are surprised every time.
What I do I build compliance into the governance model itself rather than bolting it on at the end. Every change to a regulated platform passes through defined checkpoints, security, data integrity, regulatory alignment, before it moves forward. The documentation exists continuously, not assembled the week before an audit.
How I do it I design a compliance-by-design change framework with mandatory gates aligned to the relevant regulatory standards. DORA for operational resilience, SOX where financial controls are in scope, MiFID for trading and reporting systems. The same approach I used to govern 15+ GMP-compliant platforms for Eli Lilly translates directly into financial services regulated environments.
DORA SOX Compliance MiFID Compliance-by-Design Release Governance Application Risk Register Audit Readiness
06
35%Forecast accuracy gain

Legacy systems with no plan for what comes next

What I walk into Vendor support windows have quietly closed. Systems are running on infrastructure that the business cannot easily explain to a regulator. Cloud migrations have been discussed for years but never properly sequenced or costed. Leadership keeps approving spend on legacy maintenance without understanding the alternative.
What I do I produce a rolling modernisation roadmap that gives the business a real picture of where each platform is heading. Every initiative in the roadmap has a business case, a cost comparison against continuing as-is, and a clear outcome. Nothing gets approved without that grounding.
How I do it For each legacy system I assess the right migration approach, lift-and-shift, re-platform, or re-architect, based on complexity and business criticality. I model the TCO against on-premise running costs using Azure and AWS tooling. The roadmap runs on a rolling three-year horizon, reviewed quarterly, so it stays connected to business priorities rather than becoming a theoretical exercise.
Platform Modernisation Cloud Migration Legacy Decommissioning TCO Modelling Azure / AWS API-First Strategy Technical Debt Reduction
07
50+Blockers resolved

Business and IT are pulling in different directions

What I walk into The business has a set of priorities. IT has its own view of what is feasible. Somewhere in between, the actual delivery plans bear little resemblance to either. Both sides feel unheard. Meetings happen but decisions do not. The roadmap exists on paper but nobody trusts it.
What I do I sit between both sides and make sure translation actually happens, business priorities get turned into concrete delivery plans, and technical constraints get explained in terms of business impact. I set up a shared governance forum where both sides are in the room at the same time, with a single portfolio view and a clear model for who makes which decisions.
How I do it I run alignment workshops with both business and IT leadership to surface where the real gaps are, usually they are smaller than people think, but nobody has had the conversation explicitly. The shared portfolio roadmap lives in Confluence and is visualised in Power BI so both audiences are looking at the same data. A stakeholder communication plan ensures reporting is tailored to each level, operational detail for delivery teams, strategic summary for leadership.
Business-IT Alignment Central Orchestration Governance Forum Decision Rights Model Stakeholder Communication Plan Portfolio Roadmap
08
80%Efficiency gain

Leadership is making decisions without real data

What I walk into Portfolio reporting consists of a slide deck assembled the day before a leadership meeting. The data in it is already out of date. Risks get raised when they have already become problems. Nobody has a live picture of what is on track, what is at risk, where the money is going, or what the compliance exposure looks like across the full estate.
What I do I build a live reporting layer that gives leadership a continuous view of the portfolio, delivery status, platform health, budget versus actuals, and risk exposure, without anyone having to manually pull it together. At Almaviva this improved operational visibility by 80% and at Eli Lilly it improved leadership visibility by 40%.
How I do it I connect Power BI to the live data sources. Jira for delivery, ServiceNow for platform health, Azure DevOps for release cadence, so the dashboards update automatically. Platform owners contribute a weekly health scorecard. The monthly executive review uses a standardised pack that covers delivery, cost, risk, and people on a single page per workstream, so the conversation is about decisions rather than status updates.
